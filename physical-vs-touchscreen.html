Physical Buttons vs Touchscreen: Why Tactile Controls Improve Driving Safety

Over the past decade, the automotive industry has been on an increasingly aggressive campaign to eliminate physical buttons from vehicle interiors. Dashboard screens have grown from 8 inches to over 17 inches, and the number of physical buttons has dropped by more than 60% across major manufacturers. Now, mounting evidence from human factors research, regulatory bodies, and safety organizations is pushing back against this trend.

This article examines the scientific evidence, compares the data, and explains why physical controls represent a measurable safety advantage over touchscreen alternatives in automotive applications.

Why Touchscreens Fail the Safety Test

Touchscreens create what human factors researchers call "cognitive tunneling" — the driver must: (1) visually locate the control on a screen, (2) calculate the touch target coordinates, (3) look away from the road to execute the touch, (4) verify the action completed. Each step adds time and risk.

Physical buttons eliminate this cognitive load. After brief familiarization, drivers operate tactile controls through muscle memory — the same way they use the steering wheel, pedals, and mirrors. The hand finds the button without looking, and the eyes stay on the road.
